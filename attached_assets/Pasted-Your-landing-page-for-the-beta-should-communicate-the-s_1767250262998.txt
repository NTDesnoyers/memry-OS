Your landing page for the beta should communicate the **smallest, clearest version of your product that delivers real value right now**, and then briefly acknowledge the larger vision as a hypothesis you are testing.

The purpose of the page is not to impress people with how big the product will be; it is to attract the right early adopters to test whether your current MVP actually solves their problem.

For the beta, keep only three elements above the fold:

1. A specific problem statement  
   - “Relationship-based agents lose referrals and repeat business because they drop follow-ups and forget conversations.”

2. A concrete, current solution statement  
   - “Flow OS captures your key conversations, shows you who needs your attention today, and drafts your follow-ups so you don’t drop the ball.”

3. A clear call to action  
   - “Join the Founding Agent Beta” with a short explanation: limited spots, early pricing, and that the product is evolving based on their feedback.

Your feature list should describe only the **validated or currently testable behaviors**, not the whole future platform. For example:

- Relationship CRM: FORD notes + A/B/C/D segments so you always know who matters most.  
- Conversation Capture: imports from <the one or two sources that actually work now> and quick manual logging on mobile.  
- AI Follow-ups: drafts thank-you emails and check-ins from recent conversations.  
- Today View: shows overdue relationships and suggested touches.

Anything that is not part of the current, testable MVP loop should be moved into a clearly labeled “What we’re building next” or “Where this is going” section below the fold, such as:

- “Coming next for founding agents: more conversation sources (calls/texts), deeper automations, and additional CRM integrations.”

This keeps the *core* of the page aligned with the build–measure–learn loop: you are inviting people to help you test a specific solution to a specific problem, not to buy into a fully formed OS.
